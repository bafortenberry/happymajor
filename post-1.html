What sets the best students apart?

A system.

This is the only way to efficiently manage the barrage of incoming tasks and information we have constantly flying at us. In the same way that every functioning business must have a system in place to process and manage information, so do we.

"Our minds are for having ideas not for holding them."

In the book Getting Things Done the author cites the above quote to summarize the value of a system. If you've ever forgotten to reply to a friend, submit an assignment, or shown up three hours late to a final exam (like me), then it's probably due to the lack of a neat system.

"Our minds are for having ideas not for holding them."

Commitment Control Center

Think of a commitment as any thing we’ve told ourselves we’re going to do. Even if it’s just for us, it’s a commitment. We need a control center to keep tabs on all this stuff and get it into a working order. An external mind, as the name implies, will get all this random information (to-dos, events, projects, concerns) out of our minds and into a physical (journal) or digital (laptop) space. A lot of us already have some idea of how we break up information in our mind -- “Things I need from the store: x, y, z…” or “things I need to study for: x, y, z…” These mental accounts will form the organizational structure of the system.

Framework

The medium (digital or written) itself doesn’t matter – as long as it’s not our mind. The most basic pillars of the system should be a to-do list, a calendar, and a reference space. The to-do list is anything we need to get done. I mean anything. It can be writing a paper, calling a friend, or taking a vacation. Get started by collecting every single task on your mind and writing it down. This is were the categories mentioned early come in. We can break up and organize these tasks, however works best of us.

Examples of a categorical breakdown:

Classes: study for bio test, submit lab report, finish math homework

Life: buy groceries, clean the living room, call mom

OR...

We can also organize by priority (if that's easier).

For example: Urgent & Important, Urgent but Not Important, Not Urgent but Important, Not Urgent & Not Important

The calendar should include any time-specific thing or reminder. Let’s say we have to meet with an adviser on May 1st at 10 am – get it in the calendar. Let’s say we are really busy right now but want to take a weekend trip with friends this semester. Add a reminder for one month from now that says, “think about weekend trip”.

The calendar can make it look like we have a superhuman memory. How many times have we been on either side of “yeah, let’s do that I’ll text you in a few days”? How often does that pan out? If we add a reminder a few days later to text ____ we set ourselves up to actually do this.

Finally, the reference system is all the info we need that is not a task or an event. It is organized a lot like the to-do list: however works best for us. This could contain a course syllabus, a cooking recipe, a cool article to cite later, pretty much anything worth saving.

Like learning a new set of rules or a new daily schedule this system will get more comfortable with time. As we begin, focus on the one key rule followed by nearly all effective students.

Write down everything!
