What's Your Happiness IQ?

Happiness is a surprising science. It turns out that what we think makes us happy often doesn’t and vice-versa.

Experts in the field use the term “miswanting” (affective forecasting in academic jargon) to describe the phenomena that we tend to inaccurately predict what is going to make us happy or unhappy. In short – we all need to improve our happiness IQs.

Do you know what the single most important variable in predicting longevity and life satisfaction is? (Waldinger et. al.)

Relationships.

An 80+ year study at Harvard found that quality relationships are the key to health and happiness. Wealth, career success, and social status have little impact on health and happiness. What really matters? Strong social connection. Watch Waldinger’s TED talk or read the one minute summary in top happiness TED talks.

A study (Levine et. al.) found that students thought they would be 6% happier or 43% less happy when they received a grade better or worse than expected. What was the actual impact on happiness?

Neither outcome effected happiness more than 2%.

The students were wrong - grades (good or bad) have little impact on happiness. This is a prime example of “mispredicting” what makes us happy.

In one study (Dunn et. al.) groups received $5 or $20 which could be spent to "treat themselves" or spent on someone else. Which group showed the largest increase in happiness?

Tie - $5 and $20 to spend on someone else.

We tend to think that we’re happier when we spend money on ourselves and that in either case $20 would go farther than $5. The key takeaway - it's not about how much you spend, but how you spend it.

When choosing to spend money on a material good or experience, those who chose the experience were about (Van Boven and Gilovech)…

15% happier.

Interestingly, those who spent it on experience were also perceived as more well-adjusted and conscientious by their peers. Psychologists attribute this result to the fact that enjoyment of material goods fades over time while the memory of an experience retains its positive affect. See the study.

Do you know the correlation between income/salary and happiness? (Kahneman and Krueger)?

Happiness rises then levels off as income rises

Money does increase happiness to a point; however, the effects are minimal after about $85,000 (adjusted from 75k at the time of the study). Watch Kahneman’s TED talk or read the one minute summary in top happiness TED talks.

Research in positive psychology (Lyubomirsky) has shown that what percent of happiness is under our control? i.e. not dependent upon genetic set-point or external conditions

40%

Current research states that happiness is broken-down as follows - 10% external conditions, 50% genetic set-point, and 40% intentional effort.
